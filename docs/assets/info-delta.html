Invited lecture · 22 May 2026 · Bucharest

How deltas work — the processes that shape them

A delta is where a river delivers its sediment to the sea. The contest between river discharge, waves and tides decides a delta's shape, where it grows and how it survives in a warming world. Highlights from Edward Anthony's opening lecture, in preparation for hands-on work with data.

Lecture by Prof. Edward Anthony · CEREGE, Aix-Marseille Université & ICUB, University of Bucharest

01 · River · Sea

Deltas are where a river meets the sea

Deltas are 3D coastal accumulations of predominantly fluvial-origin sediment, built at river mouths — and the most important link in the global source-to-sink system, which transports sediment from continents through rivers to the ocean.

The planview shape of deltas emerges from the balance of three forces — river, waves, tides. Anthony prefers "influence" over "dominance" (after Galloway, 1975): real deltas sit on a continuum, not in three rigid boxes.

River influence

Built by the river

Strong fluvial input and subaqueous levees push the delta seaward, into a "bird-foot" (e.g. Mississippi).

Wave influence

Smoothed by waves

Waves redistribute the sediment into barriers and spits — the Danube's Sf. Gheorghe lobe, the Rhône, the Ebro.

Tidal influence

Shaped by tides

Tidal currents and the turbidity maximum trap sediment in funnel-shaped channels (e.g. Ganges-Brahmaputra).

The mouth bar is located at the interaction. The ratio between river discharge and longshore wave transport decides whether a delta grows symmetrically and prominently or is deflected and smoothed down-drift.

Anthony, 2015, Marine Geology · conceptual model

This qualitative picture is now becoming quantitative through numerical modelling. Delta shape can be understood through the parameter Si (the ratio of longshore sediment transport to the river's solid discharge), which collapses the river–wave balance into a single number (Preoteasa et al., 2018).

◀ MORE FLUVIAL SEDIMENTSi · river/wave controlSTRONGER WAVES ▶
Low Si: symmetric, protruding lobes; sediment retained at the mouth; multiple distributary branches (e.g. Danube–Chilia). High Si: deflected, straightened shorelines; sediment escapes downdrift, into spits and barriers.

The Danube as a living textbook: within the same delta, the sheltered, river-influenced Chilia lobe and the exposed, wave-influenced Sf. Gheorghe lobe sit side by side, the difference coming from Black Sea wave fetch and the heavier sediment input on Chilia (Zăinescu et al., 2019, 2024).

02 · Climate change

Global warming threatens deltas — but unevenly

Most delta plains lie below 2 m above sea level (the Danube Delta has an average elevation of just 0.6 m), so they are extremely sensitive to two pressures: relative sea-level rise and land subsidence. Survival depends as much on management and sediment supply as on the climate scenario itself.

Factor A · the sea rises

Sea-level rise

Under low-emission scenarios (SSP1-1.9/2.6), well-managed deltas can keep up. Under extreme scenarios (SSP3-7.0, SSP5-8.5), a 1–1.4 m rise produces large-scale flooding.

Factor B · the land sinks

Subsidence

Natural compaction is accelerated by humans: groundwater and hydrocarbon extraction, drainage, construction loading. The Po Delta has subsided by metres in a century due to methane extraction.

The lever we have

Management & sediment

Sediment management, subsidence control and adaptation move a delta between "threatened" and "sustainable" — human choices matter as much as the emissions trajectory.

Application · regional context

What this means for the Danube Delta and the Black Sea

Sediment deficit

Dams on the Danube (especially Iron Gates) have drastically reduced sediment supply. A "starved" delta can no longer compensate either subsidence or sea-level rise.

Shoreline erosion

The exposed Sf. Gheorghe lobe and the downdrift sectors lose sediment through longshore transport. With diminished fluvial input, the balance tips towards shoreline retreat.

Low elevation & subsidence

The delta plain is close to sea level: modest local subsidence produces marine flooding and salinisation before the global signal becomes dominant.

Black Sea: waves & storms

Limited fetch in the north (the Chilia vs. Sf. Gheorghe contrast), but storms energetically redistribute sediment. The same shorelines can accumulate during floods and erode during storms (Zăinescu et al., 2019).

The bottom line for the Danube and for the workshop: monitoring vertical land motion and sediment budgets is the first line of defence for delta survival.

03 · Other control parameters

Beyond sea level: the physical and biological parameters that climate is changing in parallel

The shape and health of a delta — and the state of the sea in front of it — are governed by a set of variables that evolve simultaneously with climate and amplify each other. Three appear explicitly as input parameters in numerical models: water discharge (Q), sediment load (Qs) and wave height (Hs).

Water discharge · Q

The hydrological regime

Earlier alpine snowmelt and more variable precipitation shift the peak and sharpen the flood ↔ drought contrast. Low discharge = less sediment delivered and a saline wedge further upstream.

Sediment load · Qs

Sediment supply

Basin erosion responds to precipitation, but on the Danube dams remain the dominant control. The result is a reduced and less predictable supply — essential for the Si balance.

Temperature · river & sea

Water is warming

The Black Sea is warming at the surface → stronger stratification, effects on oxygenation and ecosystems, marine heatwaves. Warmer river water reduces the winter ice regime on the Danube.

Salinity

Saline intrusion

Low discharge plus sea-level rise push the salt wedge upstream, into the branches and the aquifer, salinising soils and fresh water. A slow but cumulative pressure.

Wave climate · Hs

Waves & storms

Wave energy and direction control longshore transport and shoreline shape (the Si engine). Changes in the Black Sea storm regime can reorient the drift and accelerate erosion.

Nutrients · oxygenation

Danube → Black Sea

The Danube delivers nutrients onto the north-western shelf. Warming and stratification amplify the risk of eutrophication and hypoxia — a loop where river and sea influence each other.

Vegetation · NDVI

Reed, floating islands & biogeomorphology

Vegetation — the Danube's reed beds are among the most extensive in the world — stabilises sediment; bank vegetation enhances levee formation. NDVI tracks its vigour, phenology and extent, plus its response to flooding, drought and salinisation (the dashed curve = vegetation under stress, illustrative).

These parameters do not act in isolation: lower discharge strengthens saline intrusion, warmer water strengthens stratification and hypoxia, and a different storm regime reshuffles sediment and stresses vegetation. The effects amplify each other.

04 · Data & methods

Deltas leave signals that can be read in imagery, bathymetry, sensors and model data (waves, wind, discharge)

Deltas are dynamic, reactive systems that record their own behaviour. The methods below cover exactly the data sources from the workshop's modules — from open global datasets to field measurements — and pair naturally with numerical modelling and AI.

Satellite remote sensing

Imagery, water & vegetation

Landsat, Sentinel, MODIS, SPOT, WorldView provide morphology, shoreline dynamics, land/water mapping and spectral indices (NDVI / NDWI). SWOT (launched Nov 2022) returns water occurrence and connectivity across whole deltas.

Altimetry & subsidence

LiDAR & InSAR

Airborne and drone LiDAR (2.5–10 cm) and terrestrial laser scanning build high-resolution DEMs; SAR interferometry maps subsidence rates over decades.

Bathymetry

Underwater

Multibeam & side-scan sonar — now deployable from uncrewed surface vessels (USV) — and bathymetric LiDAR resolve mouth bars, the beach face and channel change for 3D budgets.

Field hydrodynamics

Currents, waves, turbidity

ADCP & ADV (velocity, level, backscatter→turbidity), electromagnetic current meters, wave buoys, pressure sensors and OBS measure real processes — flood vs. storm.

Hydro-meteorology & reanalysis

Discharge, wind, waves, SST

Discharge series (Q, Qs), ERA5 for wind and waves, plus sea surface temperature (SST) link climatic forcing to the delta's response — the data that "feeds" the parameters in chapter 03.

Numerical modelling

From data to process

Delft3D and MIKE 21 are numerical platforms for hydrodynamics and morphodynamics: they solve flow, sediment transport and bed evolution equations (2D/3D). They quantify the river–wave–tide contributions, channel connectivity and the response of deltas to sea-level rise.

Three takeaways for working with data

  • Deltas live on a continuum. River, waves and tides influence — they don't rigidly dominate — and the mouth bar is the template that decides shape and shoreline.
  • Climate changes more than sea level. Discharge, water temperature, salinity, wave climate, nutrients and vegetation all shift simultaneously and amplify each other. For the Danube, sediment deficit and subsidence remain the key pressures.
  • The signals can be read. Satellite databases (including NDVI), bathymetry and field sensors — together with modelling and AI — let us quantify how a delta is changing, even today.
