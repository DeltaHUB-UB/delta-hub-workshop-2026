Three questions come back over and over with environmental data: what do the data look like (the distribution), how do they change over time (trend and season) and how rare is something extreme (the return period). The five parts below answer exactly these.

Before we startcontext
Data types

What kind of data are we analysing?

Before any statistics, ask yourself what shape the data have. The shape decides how hard it is — both in volume and in code.

Almost everything we use fits into a few structures, depending on how many dimensions they have. A time series is a single point tracked over time (a sequence of numbers) — the easiest to analyse. A grid / raster is a 2D spatial matrix of values (a map); when you track it over time it becomes a 3D cube (map × time) — much harder, because both volume and code complexity grow. If you add depth or atmospheric levels, you reach a 4D field — which we won't cover today.

Structure → tool → difficulty
Time series
One point × time (GRDC discharge, a station, a cell from SST) · numpy / pandas · easy
Grid (2D)
One map, one moment (DEM, bathymetry) · xarray / rasterio · medium
Grid × time (3D)
Raster over time (ERA5, SST, NDVI, LWQ, CMIP6) · xarray + NetCDF · harder (volume)
4D field
+ depth / levels (ORAS5, CMIP6 levels) · xarray + dask · hard — not covered

The practical trick for today: from a grid you can extract a single cell (the one nearest to the Danube Delta) and you're back to a simple time series — on which everything that follows applies directly. Analysis over full grids (pixel-by-pixel trend maps) uses the same methods, just repeated over millions of cells.

Part 18 min
Level · descriptive

Descriptive statistics & distributions

First reflex with any new sequence: summarise it in a few numbers and look at its shape.

A few numbers summarise the centre, the spread and the shape. For the centre: the mean (the "centre of mass") and the median (the middle value). For spread: the standard deviation and the variance (its square), the IQR (interquartile range, Q3−Q1) and the coefficient of variation (CV = std/mean, relative spread). For shape: skewness (how tilted the distribution is) and kurtosis (how "heavy" the tails are). And percentiles (e.g. P95 = the threshold below which 95% of values lie) describe any point of the distribution.

The median, the IQR and the percentiles are called robust statistics: they aren't pulled by a few huge values. The mean, the standard deviation and especially skewness/kurtosis are, on the contrary, sensitive to extremes — a single storm wave noticeably shifts them.

But where do these numbers come from? From a population of values. Environmental data usually appear as a time series: a variable measured at successive moments (e.g. wave height, daily, over a year).

If you count how many measurements fall in each value interval, you obtain a distribution — a map of how often each value appears. The histogram is exactly that: each measurement "falls" into a column, and the column height shows how frequent that value is.

The difference between mean and median is key for environmental data, because many are asymmetric. Wave height, discharge, turbidity — all have a long tail toward large values (storms, floods) and a low peak near the small values. For such distributions the mean is larger than the median and can mislead.

Three plots tell you almost everything: the histogram (shape of the distribution), the boxplot (median, quartiles, outliers) and the ECDF (cumulative proportion — you read directly "what fraction of the time the wave exceeds 2 m").

Many environmental variables follow known distributions, and recognising which is useful: it tells you which statistics make sense and is the basis of the extreme analysis in Part 4.

Useful distributions in environmental data
Normal
Symmetric, "bell-shaped". Typical for anomalies and measurement errors.
Log-normal
Positive, right-skewed. Discharge, turbidity, concentrations, sediment sizes.
Weibull / Rayleigh
Wave height and wind speed.
Gamma
Precipitation amounts.
Exponential / Pareto
Exceedances over a threshold and inter-event times (see Part 4).

How do you check that a distribution "fits"? Visually with a QQ-plot (data quantiles vs. theoretical quantiles — if they lie on the diagonal, it fits), and numerically with a Kolmogorov-Smirnov or Anderson-Darling test.

Why it matters for the delta To understand the erosive effects of waves you don't care about the mean wave but about the high percentiles (P95, P99) of wave height. Extreme events count disproportionately more! Report median + IQR, not mean ± standard deviation, when the distribution is asymmetric.
Python code — summary & distributionPython 
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# vhm0 = pandas.Series with significant wave height (m), time-indexed
print(vhm0.describe())              # count, mean, std, min, 25/50/75%, max
print("median:", vhm0.median())
print("P95:", vhm0.quantile(0.95))
print("P99:", vhm0.quantile(0.99))

fig, ax = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(12, 3.5))
vhm0.hist(bins=40, ax=ax[0]); ax[0].set_title("Histogram")
ax[1].boxplot(vhm0.dropna()); ax[1].set_title("Boxplot")

# ECDF: what fraction of the time the wave is below a given value
x = np.sort(vhm0.dropna().values)
y = np.arange(1, len(x)+1) / len(x)
ax[2].plot(x, y); ax[2].set_title("ECDF")
plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()

# fit a distribution and check with a QQ-plot + KS test
from scipy import stats
par = stats.lognorm.fit(vhm0.dropna())        # e.g. log-normal
stats.probplot(vhm0.dropna(), dist=stats.lognorm,
               sparams=par, plot=plt)              # QQ-plot
D, p = stats.kstest(vhm0.dropna(), "lognorm", par)
print(f"KS: D={D:.3f}, p={p:.3g}")         # large p = good fit

Time series: climatology, season, anomalies

Environmental data almost always have an annual rhythm. You have to separate it from real change.

Three related concepts: climatology is the long-term average for each month (or day of year) — "what a typical February looks like". The seasonal cycle is exactly this recurring pattern. The anomaly is what remains after you subtract the climatology: anomaly = value − the average of that month. Anomalies are where you see trends and events, without the season hiding them.

Also useful: the rolling mean: it smooths day-to-day noise so you can see the pattern. Careful, though — smoothing doesn't "create" information, it just makes it more readable.

All of this boils down to a single idea: any environmental series can be decomposed into three pieces — the slow trend, the recurring seasonal cycle, and the rest (noise + events). That is what the STL method from the extended toolkit does.

Why it matters for the delta Danube discharge has a strong season (the spring flood). If you compare March directly to August you will "see" a drop that is just season. Work on anomalies. Likewise, a marine heatwave shows clearly as an SST anomaly, not in the raw series.
Python code — climatology & anomaliesPython 
# sst = pandas.Series monthly (°C) with a DatetimeIndex
clim = sst.groupby(sst.index.month).transform("mean")  # monthly climatology
anom = sst - clim                                       # SST anomaly

anom.plot(title="SST anomaly relative to the monthly climatology")

# 12-month rolling mean to see the smooth trend
anom.rolling(12, center=True).mean().plot()

Level · relationships & change

Trends & correlations

Now we ask: is something growing over time? and do two things move together?

The simplest trend estimator is linear regression: you fit the best straight line through the data, and its slope is the change per unit of time (mm/yr, °C/decade). Easy to compute, but sensitive to outliers.

The slope tells you how much things change, but not how well the line describes the data. For that we use R² (the coefficient of determination): the fraction of the data's variance explained by the model, between 0 and 1. R² = 0.9 means the line explains 90% of the spread; R² = 0.1 means 90% remains noise. For simple linear regression, R² is just the square of the Pearson correlation.

Here lies the subtle and very common pitfall in environmental data: significance (p) and strength (R²) are different things. With long series you can obtain a "significant" trend (small p) that still explains only a few percent of variability (small R²). Both panels below have the same slope and can both be "significant" — but confidence in them is radically different.

The other half of the story is the p-value (or p-score): the probability of obtaining a slope at least as large if in fact there is no trend (the null hypothesis). In other words: how often would you see "accidentally" a slope like yours if you shuffled the data in time? Small p = hard to explain by chance (so the slope is probably real). The usual threshold is p < 0.05. Caution: small p ≠ important trend — over long series, even a tiny slope becomes "significant".

The third metric is RMSE (root-mean-square error): the typical prediction error, in the real units of the variable (cm, °C, m³/s). Computed as RMSE = √(mean of residuals²) — that is, an average of the vertical distances between the real data and the model line. Unlike R² (unitless, abstract), RMSE is directly interpretable: "my prediction is off by ±X cm on average". It's useful when you want to size the margin of error of a model.

For environmental data we often prefer a robust, non-parametric duo: the Mann-Kendall test says whether there is a monotonic trend (without assuming linearity or normality), and the Sen slope (Theil-Sen) estimates how large it is, resistant to outliers. It's the standard in hydrology and climatology.

When you want to see whether two variables move together, you use correlation: the Pearson coefficient measures the linear relationship, and Spearman (on ranks) measures any monotonic relationship — better for asymmetric data. Remember the golden rule: correlation does not imply causation.

Why it matters for the delta Sea-level trend (mm/yr) from altimetry, NDVI greening per decade, or the SST slope — all require Mann-Kendall + Sen, not just a line drawn by eye. And the discharge–turbidity correlation is better measured with Spearman, because both are strongly asymmetric.
Python code — regression, Mann-Kendall, correlationsPython 
from scipy import stats
import numpy as np

# --- linear regression (simple trend) ---
t = np.arange(len(sla))                      # sla = sea level (cm)
res = stats.linregress(t, sla.values)
print(f"slope = {res.slope*12:.2f} cm/yr, p = {res.pvalue:.3g}")
print(f"R² = {res.rvalue**2:.2f}")        # share of variance explained by the trend
pred = res.intercept + res.slope*t
rmse = np.sqrt(np.mean((sla.values-pred)**2))
print(f"RMSE = {rmse:.2f} cm")         # typical error, in real units

# --- Mann-Kendall + Sen slope (robust) ---
import pymannkendall as mk     # pip install pymannkendall
out = mk.original_test(sla.values)
print(out.trend, "| Sen slope:", out.slope, "| p:", out.p)

# --- correlations ---
r_p, _ = stats.pearsonr(discharge, turbidity)   # linear
r_s, _ = stats.spearmanr(discharge, turbidity)  # monotonic, robust
print(f"Pearson={r_p:.2f}  Spearman={r_s:.2f}")
Three numbers, three different questions
p
Does a trend exist? — the probability that a slope at least as large would appear by pure chance if no trend really existed. Small p (e.g. <0.05) = hard to attribute to chance. It does not say how strong the trend is.
R²
How much does it matter? — the fraction of variance explained by the model (0–1). Computation: R² = 1 − SS_res/SS_tot.
RMSE
How far are the predictions? — the typical model error, in real units (cm, °C…). Computation: RMSE = √(mean of residuals²). Unlike R² (unitless), RMSE is directly interpretable.

The pitfall to remember: small p does not mean an important trend. With decades of data, even a tiny slope becomes "statistically significant" (small p), but if R² is small and RMSE is large, the trend explains very little and the prediction remains weak. Always report all three.

Level · extremes

Percentiles, extremes & return periods

For the coast, means matter less than extremes. The real question: how big is the wave that occurs once in 100 years?

We start with a bridge concept: the quantile. A percentile is just a quantile in percent — P95 is the value below which 95% of the data lie. On the cumulative plot (CDF/ECDF) you read it the other way around: pick a probability on the vertical axis, go across to the curve, and drop down to the value. This operation — "from probability to value" — is exactly what a return period does.

Same idea, three names
Percentile
P95 = the threshold exceeded 5% of the time.
Quantile
Same value, expressed as a probability (0.95).
Return level
The quantile at p = 1 − 1/T of the annual maxima: T=100 → quantile 0.99.

Quantiles also have a role you've already met: quantile mapping, the bias-correction method that aligns a model's quantiles with the observed ones. Still percentiles, different use.

Now the extremes themselves. A return period of 100 years means an event with a 1% probability of being exceeded in any given year — not "exactly once every 100 years". The corresponding level is called the return level.

Two classical approaches. Block maxima: take the maximum of each year and fit a GEV distribution (Generalized Extreme Value). Peaks-over-threshold: take all the peaks above a high threshold and fit a GPD distribution (Generalized Pareto) — uses more data but requires choosing a threshold. For a quick start, the empirical return period (from the rank of the values) is enough to grasp the idea.

Why it matters for the delta The 100-year wave height and the extreme sea level are design parameters for coastal protection and for hazard maps. Caution: extrapolating to 100 years from a series of a few decades has large uncertainty — that is why you always report a confidence interval, not a single figure.
Python code — annual maxima & GEVPython 
from scipy import stats
import numpy as np

# vhm0 = hourly series of wave height (m)
maxima = vhm0.resample("YE").max().dropna()   # one maximum per year

# fit a GEV distribution (block maxima)
shape, loc, scale = stats.genextreme.fit(maxima.values)

def return_level(T):
    p = 1 - 1.0/T                              # non-exceedance probability
    return stats.genextreme.ppf(p, shape, loc, scale)

for T in (10, 50, 100):
    print(f"H_s at {T} years = {return_level(T):.2f} m")

# EMPIRICAL return period (quick intuition, no fit):
x = np.sort(maxima.values)[::-1]
rank = np.arange(1, len(x)+1)
T_emp = (len(x)+1) / rank          # Weibull formula

# confidence interval via bootstrap (uncertainty matters!)
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
boot = []
for _ in range(1000):
    s = rng.choice(maxima.values, len(maxima), replace=True)
    f, l, sc = stats.genextreme.fit(s)
    boot.append(stats.genextreme.ppf(0.99, f, l, sc))
lo, hi = np.percentile(boot, [2.5, 97.5])
print(f"H_s 100 yr: 95% CI = [{lo:.2f}, {hi:.2f}] m")

Level · statistical hygiene

Common pitfalls

Almost all mistakes in environmental data analysis boil down to eight. Recognise them and you've won half the battle.

01Autocorrelation

Consecutive values (daily, monthly) resemble each other, so the "effective" number of independent observations is smaller than it looks. That overestimates the significance of a trend: a small p can be illusory. Use the effective number of degrees of freedom or tests that account for autocorrelation (e.g. modified Mann-Kendall).

02Non-normality

The mean, the standard deviation and the t-test assume symmetric distributions. Waves, discharge and turbidity are not. Prefer the median, percentiles and robust or non-parametric methods (Spearman, Sen).

03Multiple testing

If you compute the trend in 10,000 pixels, ~500 will look "significant" at p<0.05 by chance alone. Correct (False Discovery Rate / Benjamini-Hochberg) when making gridded trend maps.

04Trend vs. noise

A short series plus large natural variability can "look like" a trend that doesn't exist. You need long series — hence the value of multi-decadal reanalyses. Don't extrapolate a trend from 5 years.

05One model is not the truth

In CMIP6, the spread between models is often larger than the difference between scenarios in the short term. Report the ensemble median + interval, never a single model as a certainty.

06Gaps & missing values

SST, NDVI and Lake Water Quality have gaps (clouds). Don't naively interpolate over long gaps and don't confuse a _FillValue with a real zero — always check the data mask.

07Data errors & insufficient filtering

Drifting sensors, wrong units, real outliers (a storm) vs. artefacts (an instrument spike). Don't run statistics "blind": check the distribution, look at min/max, exclude physically impossible values. A single -9999 left in the series wrecks the mean, standard deviation and trend. Plot before you compute.

08Statistics not suited to the distribution

Using statistics designed for the wrong kind of distribution: the mean (and standard deviation) when the distribution is asymmetric or heavy-tailed, instead of median + IQR. For discharge, waves, turbidity — extremes pull the mean up, and the "typical value" becomes a fiction. Choose your statistic after looking at the histogram, not before.

Extended toolkitreference

Further — other useful methods

Beyond the five parts, here are the methods you'll most often encounter on delta data. You can tackle them in the AI-assisted coding session: you understand when they're used, and you ask the assistant for the code.

Uncertainty

Bootstrap

Resample the data with replacement thousands of times and recompute the statistic — you get confidence intervals without assuming a distribution.

For you: error bars on trends, means and return levels. scipy.stats.bootstrap
Comparisons

Hypothesis tests

Compare two groups: t-test if the data are ~normal, Mann-Whitney U otherwise. Is the observed difference real or by chance?

For you: before/after an intervention, two delta branches, two periods. scipy.stats
Periodicities

Autocorrelation & spectral

The ACF shows how much the series "remembers" its past; the periodogram / FFT extracts hidden cycles (season, seiches, tides).

For you: rhythms in discharge, water level, waves. statsmodels.acf, numpy.fft
Decomposition

STL

Splits a series into trend + season + residual, robust to outliers. The formal version of Part 2.

For you: isolate the change signal in SST or discharge. statsmodels STL
Trend in extremes

Quantile regression

Estimates the trend not of the mean but of a percentile (e.g. P95). Climate change often affects extremes more than the mean.

For you: "is the extreme wave growing, not just the mean one?" statsmodels QuantReg
Spatio-temporal fields

PCA / EOF

Reduce a gridded field to a few dominant modes — the spatial patterns that explain most of the variance.

For you: dominant modes in SST or sea-level fields. sklearn.PCA, eofs
Grouping

Clustering

Group pixels or observations into similar regimes — k-means (hard membership) or fuzzy c-means (gradual membership).

For you: turbidity regimes, delta classification. sklearn, scikit-fuzzy
Spatial

Interpolation & kriging

From sparse measurements to a continuous map, with point-wise uncertainty — superior to simple interpolation.

For you: maps from sediment samples or sparse bathymetry. pykrige, scipy.griddata

Advanced time series

Memory, cycles & regime shifts

Three tools that squeeze far more out of a time series than the mean and the trend.

A1Spectral & wavelet analysis

Any hidden rhythm shows in frequency. The power spectrum (FFT / periodogram) shows which cycles dominate — annual, half-yearly, tidal. Wavelet goes further: it shows when each cycle appears, useful for non-stationary signals (a storm event, a shifting regime).

Why it matters for the delta For discharge and water level, the spectrum confirms the annual cycle; the wavelet captures short events (floods, storms) that a global spectrum would dilute.
Code — spectrum & waveletPython 
import numpy as np

# synthetic example: three sinusoids + noise
t = np.linspace(0, 1, 1000)
x = (18*np.sin(2*np.pi*3*t)
     + 11*np.sin(2*np.pi*7*t)
     +  7*np.sin(2*np.pi*13*t)
     + np.random.normal(0, 3, t.size))

freq = np.fft.rfftfreq(t.size, d=t[1]-t[0])
power = np.abs(np.fft.rfft(x - x.mean()))**2
# the spectrum peaks fall at 3, 7 and 13

# continuous wavelet for non-stationary signals:
import pywt
coef, _ = pywt.cwt(x, np.arange(1,128), "morl")
A2Cross-correlation (lead-lag)

Two series can be linked, but shifted. Cross-correlation (CCF) looks for the lag at which the correlation is maximum — that is, by how much one signal lags the other.

Why it matters for the delta The chain precipitation → discharge → turbidity has lags of days. CCF gives you exactly that delay, essential for linking causes to effects.
Code — cross-correlationPython 
from scipy import signal
ccf = signal.correlate(turbid - turbid.mean(), discharge - discharge.mean(), mode="full")
lags = signal.correlation_lags(len(turbid), len(discharge))
lag_max = lags[ccf.argmax()]   # lag with maximum correlation
A4Change-point detection

Sometimes there is no smooth trend but a rupture: the mean or the slope changes abruptly at a given moment. Tests like Pettitt or CUSUM find that point.

Why it matters for the delta The construction of a dam or a shift in sedimentary regime often appears as a break point, not as a trend. Detecting it tells you when the system changed.
Code — change-pointPython 
import ruptures as rpt     # pip install ruptures
algo = rpt.Pelt(model="rbf").fit(series.values)
points = algo.predict(pen=10)   # indices of the breaks

Coastal & extreme statistics

Direction & wave climate

Two methods tailored to marine data — exactly your terrain.

B1Wave rose (circular statistics)

Direction is circular data: 359° and 1° are neighbours, so the arithmetic mean makes no sense (the vector mean is used). The wave rose shows which directions waves come from and with what intensity.

Why it matters for the delta The dominant wave direction controls longshore sediment transport and the shape of the mouths. For a wave-influenced delta, the rose is the first figure to put on the table.
Code — rose & circular meanPython 
from windrose import WindroseAxes   # pip install windrose
ax = WindroseAxes.from_ax()
ax.bar(direction, hs, normed=True, bins=[0,1,2,3])
# circular mean:
from scipy.stats import circmean
dir_mean = circmean(direction, high=360)
B2Hs–Tp diagram (2D density)

Wave climate isn't just height, it's the height–period pair. A 2D density (or scatter) shows which combinations occur most often — the basis for computing energy and loading on structures.

Why it matters for the delta The same height with a longer period carries much more energy to the shore. The Hs–Tp diagram is the standard in coastal engineering.
Code — 2D densityPython 
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
plt.hexbin(tp, hs, gridsize=30, cmap="viridis")   # or seaborn.kdeplot(x=tp, y=hs)
Advanced · Creference
Spatial methods

Patterns in space: EOF / PCA

When the data are gridded fields, statistics can extract the dominant spatial patterns.

C1EOF / PCA on fields

A gridded field (SST, sea level) varies in time and space. EOF (PCA on geophysical data) decomposes it into a few dominant modes: each has a fixed spatial pattern and a time series that says how active that pattern is.

Why it matters for the delta A few EOF modes summarise a whole SST or sea-level field of the Black Sea — useful for linking regional variability to forcings (wind, discharge).
Code — EOFPython 
from eofs.xarray import Eof   # pip install eofs
solver = Eof(sst_anom)
modes = solver.eofs(neofs=3)        # the spatial patterns
pcs = solver.pcs(npcs=3)            # the time series
